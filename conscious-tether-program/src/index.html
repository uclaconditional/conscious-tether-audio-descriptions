Conscious Tether: Art and the Internet in Los Angeles

is a group exhibition of contemporary artists considering what it means to live with and through the internet.

Appearing to be both nowhere and everywhere at once, the internet’s presence is always felt, always there, always just outside the bounds of perception. Parallel to the common perception within media theory that infrastructures and apparatuses only make themselves known during instances of breakdown or failure, Conscious Tether feels out the sites at which matter, energy, and labor assert themselves amidst conditions of invisibility, control, and waste. These artists prize apart how the networked, digitized subject might respond to the state of ceaseless onlineness; at the same time, the exhibition also asks what role the real space of Los Angeles plays within these complex interactions.

The internet’s inaugural message sent from UCLA’s Boelter Hall reached its destination, but was incomplete. The four-node network crashed after the first two letters—LO—of “LOGIN.” This initial semi-failure or partial transmission seems in retrospect, given the intervening histories and usages, emblematic of all that is in excess of the internet and yet could no longer exist without it.

The assembled artists in Conscious Tether emphasize the material and immaterial reminders of the internet’s unceasing presence, exploring its seeming peripheries. American Artist, ann haeyoung, and Romi Morrison implicate systems of quantification, control, and extraction; LA Cryptoparty, KCHUNG, Danielle Dean, Tiny Tech Zines (Rachel Simanjuntak, Jules Kris, Tyler Yin, Tristan Espinoza), and Xin Xin consider practices of labor, privacy, and consent; while works by Ahree Lee, Devin Kenny, and Alice Yuan Zhang reveal both the realities and possible futures of gendered technology, encryption, and technological ruins. Conscious Tether celebrates and acknowledges these peculiar intransigences, to stay with the trouble that rubs up against the gridded globe of the “World Wide Web.”

Conscious Tether features work by Ahree Lee, Alice Yuan Zhang, American Artist, ann haeyoung, Danielle Dean, Devin Kenny, KCHUNG, LA Cryptoparty, Romi Morrison, Tiny Tech Zines (Rachel Simanjuntak, Jules Kris, Tyler Yin, Tristan Espinoza), and Xin Xin.

Co-curated by Chandler McWilliams and Audrey Min, with public programming organized by Daniel Soto and Casey Reas.

  • Ahree Lee is a multi-disciplinary artist working in video, new media, and textiles. Lee received her M.F.A. in graphic design from Yale School of Art. Her work has been commissioned by Asian Art Museum, San Francisco; Irish Museum of Modern Art; Cantor Arts Center, Stanford University; Los Angeles Municipal Art Gallery; 01SJ Biennial, San Jose; International Short Film Festival, Leuven, Belgium; the International Festival of Video Art of Casablanca; and the Sundance Channel.
  • Alice Yuan Zhang is a Chinese- American media artist, researcher, and cultural organizer currently living in Los Angeles. Her transdisciplinary practice bridges ecology and technology, working to empower decolonial praxis and heal alongside marginalized communities through ancestral remembering, interspecies pedagogy, and networked solidarity. Zhang has participated in artist residencies at Moos.garden and CultureHub and is a founding steward of virtual care lab and a community member of NAVEL Los Angeles.
  • American Artist makes thought experiments that mine the history of technology, race, and knowledge production, beginning with their legal name change in 2013. Their artwork primarily takes the form of sculpture, software, and video. Artist has exhibited at the Whitney Museum of American Art; the Museum of Modern Art; Studio Museum in Harlem; Museum of Contemporary Art Chicago; Kunsthalle Basel; and Nam June Paik Art Center, Seoul.
  • ann haeyoung is an artist and writer who creates stories about the human and material costs of technological production. They take visual cues from science fiction and horror, and use the inefficient, uncanny, and surreal to counteract dominant narratives of technological progress and neoliberal futurity. ann received their MFA from UCLA in 2023, and their work has been exhibited at Roswell Museum, New Mexico; The Mistake Room, Los Angeles; and New Wight Gallery, Los Angeles.
  • Working with archives, video, performance, social practice, sculpture, and drawing, Danielle A. Dean investigates the recursive loops between the circulation of ideas and the material reproduction of global capitalism. Dean received an MFA from California Institute of the Arts and is an alumna of the Whitney Independent Study Program. Recently completed projects include new commissions for Mercer Union, Toronto (2024) and Performa, New York (2021), and a solo exhibition at Tate Britain, London (2022).
  • Devin Kenny is an artist, writer, and musician, and a dPhil Candidate in Fine Art at the University of Oxford. Born on the South Side of Chicago, Kenny relocated to New York City as a teenager. Kenny received a Master of Fine Arts from University of California, Los Angeles, in addition to attending the Whitney Independent Study Program in New York.Their work has been exhibited at institutions including MoMA PS1, The Kitchen, Performance Space, REDCAT, and Queens Museum.
  • KCHUNG Radio has collaborated with cultural organizations at all scales, including Made in LA 2014, Ooga Booga, Thank You For Coming, Perform Chinatown, MOCA, Human Resources, LA Zine Fest, Night Gallery, The Smell, Pehrspace, and LACMA. KCHUNG Radio is a Creative Capital awardee, and has received generous funding from Los Angeles County Department of Arts and Culture, the Museum of Contemporary Art Los Angeles, and the greater KCHUNG community.
  • LA Cryptoparty is a group of friends who host workshops on privacy, security, and anonymity to participants of all skill levels. LA Cryptoparty was founded in 2014 by No Name, Professor Y, and Taze.
  • Romi Ron Morrison is an interdisciplinary artist and scholar. Using maps, data, sound, performance, and video, their installations center Black diasporic technologies that challenge the demands of an increasingly quantified world—reducing land into property, people into digits, and knowledge into data. Romi has exhibited work and lectured at institutions including Transmediale, Berlin; The Kitchen, New York; ALT_CPH Biennial, Copenhagen; and the Museum of Contemporary Art Chicago.
  • Tiny Tech Zines (TTZ) is an artist-run tech zine fair, distro, and collective based in Los Angeles. They use zines and critical gatherings to explore the relationships between marginalized communities and technology. TTZ is co-organized by Rachel Simanjuntak, Jules Kris, Tyler Yin, and Tristan Espinoza.
  • Xin Xin is a Taiwanese-American artist exploring the creative potential of community-driven technology. As creator of TogetherNet and co-curator of the Critical Coding Cookbook, Xin facilitates consensus and social engagement in digital spaces. Their work has been exhibited internationally at Ars Electronica, Human Resources, Z/KU, and Kunstverein Wolfsburg.
